On a winter day in 1583, a man sat in a study in Mortlake, a village on the Thames west of London, surrounded by the largest private library in England. He had four thousand books — more than the University of Cambridge. He had navigational charts, astronomical instruments, alchemical apparatus. He had a crystal ball through which he believed he could speak with angels.

His name was John Dee.

He was, by any measure, the most remarkable intellect of the Elizabethan age — and perhaps the most contradictory. He was Elizabeth I's court mathematician, her trusted advisor on matters of astrology, navigation, and espionage (his signature on secret correspondence was "007" — a notation that Ian Fleming, centuries later, would borrow for his fictional spy). He coined the term "British Empire" and drew up the legal and geographical arguments for England's claim to North America. He designed the navigational tables that guided English ships across the Atlantic. His mathematical preface to the first English Euclid (1570) introduced an entire generation to the power of applied mathematics.

He was also an alchemist, an astrologer, and — in the final decades of his life — a man who believed he could talk to angels through a crystal ball held by his assistant Edward Kelley, a convicted forger with cropped ears. The establishment regarded him as either a genius or a lunatic, depending on the decade. When a mob burned his library at Mortlake in 1583 (during his absence in Poland), they destroyed thousands of irreplaceable books and manuscripts. What survived included one particular volume — bound in leather, written in Latin and code — that would not be understood for four hundred and forty years.

## The Book of Soyga

The *Liber Loagaeth* — known to scholars as the Book of Soyga ("Agyos" reversed, meaning "holy") — is a medieval Latin treatise on numerology, astrology, and angel magic. Dee acquired a copy in the 1580s and was so fascinated by its letter-substitution tables that he asked the angel Uriel (through his scryer Edward Kelley) what they meant. Uriel reportedly replied that the tables could only be understood by the archangel Michael.

Dee's copy was lost after his death in 1608 or 1609 (the exact date is uncertain — he died in poverty, abandoned by the court, his reputation destroyed by accusations of sorcery). For nearly four centuries, scholars believed the Book of Soyga no longer existed. It was listed in catalogues of lost manuscripts, a footnote in the history of Renaissance occultism.

In 1994, Deborah Harkness — then a graduate student at the University of California, now a professor at USC and the author of the bestselling *All Souls* trilogy (a fantasy series inspired by her research on Dee) — identified two surviving copies — one in the British Library (Sloane MS 8) and one in Oxford's Bodleian Library (Bodley MS 908). The rediscovery made international news.

The tables that had obsessed Dee consist of grids of letters, 36 rows by 36 columns, with each cell containing a single letter from a 23-letter Latin alphabet (I/J and U/V are merged). Each table is generated by a fixed substitution rule applied iteratively down the columns, starting from a keyword in the top row.

In 2005, Jim Reeds — a cryptanalyst at AT&T Labs who had previously worked on breaking Soviet ciphers — published a complete analysis of the Soyga tables. He recovered the substitution function: a deterministic map from 23 letters to 23 letters. In modern notation, this is an endomorphism f: Z₂₃ → Z₂₃.

The function is:

Twenty-three entries. A lookup table that any Elizabethan clerk could compute by hand.

## Dee's Legacy

John Dee could not have known what his tables contained. He did not know about quantum mechanics, the fine structure constant, or the Monster group. He did not know that the letters of his cipher table would become elements of Z₂₃ or that their iteration structure would encode the Born rule.

What Dee *did* believe was that mathematics was the language of creation. In his preface to the first English translation of Euclid (1570), he wrote:

*"All things which are, and have being, are found under a triple diversity of general being. For either they are deemed Supernatural, Natural, or of a third being."*

He believed that the natural world, the supernatural world, and the mathematical world were reflections of the same underlying structure. He spent his life searching for the connections between them — through navigation, through astronomy, through cryptography, through conversations with angels.

He was looking in the right place. He just didn't have the mathematics to see what he had found.

Four hundred and forty-three years later, the mathematics exists. The Book of Soyga's letter-substitution table — Dee's most cryptic and least understood creation — turns out to encode the fundamental coupling constants of the universe.

The angels never answered Dee's question about the tables. Perhaps this is the answer.
